Making raffles a real business type on Whop.

How much of a brand-new business type can we stand up on the APIs we already have — and where do we have to build?

Someone has one Charizard PSA 10, a spare e-bike, or a box of Labubus, and they want to raffle it: sell 1,000 tickets at $5, draw a winner live, ship the prize. On paper that's a tiny product. In practice it asks a sharp question of our platform — how much of a brand-new business type can we stand up on the APIs we already have, and where do we have to build?

We traced the seller's path call by call against the live codebase. The encouraging headline: roughly three-quarters of the journey is reuse. The honest headline: the quarter that's missing is the quarter that makes a raffle a raffle.

The surface we're building on

Whop exposes its public API two ways, both backed by one internal GraphQL core. There's a native /api/v1 layer of hand-written Rails controllers (products, plans, accounts, users, checkout configurations), and a GraphQL→REST proxy that auto-translates ~59 resources into REST for free (companies, files, affiliates, account links, webhooks, shipments, and more). Everything public is bundled into the generated @whop/sdk — 65 resources today. None of them is a raffle.

That distinction matters because "add an endpoint" isn't one task. It's a choice between a native controller, a proxy resource, or a whop.com-only internal mutation that never ships to developers at all. The raffle journey touches all three.

What already works

Most of the scaffolding a raffle needs is just commerce, and Whop is good at commerce. The seller can:

  • create an account and a company (accounts.create, companies.create);
  • upload a prize photo to S3 through the Files module and attach it;
  • create a product with collect_shipping_address flipped on for physical prizes;
  • create a one-time plan with stock: 1000 — a fixed-price, limited-supply "ticket";
  • mint a shareable checkout link and recruit affiliates on a revenue share;
  • onboard for payouts, receive entry webhooks, and ship the prize with EasyPost-backed tracking.
// a "ticket" today: a capped one-time plan
POST /api/v1/plans
{
  "product_id": "prod_…",
  "plan_type": "one_time",
  "initial_price": "5",
  "currency": "usd",
  "stock": 1000
}

Run down that list and it reads like a working raffle. It isn't — it's a working store that sells 1,000 identical $5 items. The difference is everything a buyer cares about.

Where it breaks

Three things make a raffle a raffle, and Whop models none of them today.

1. A deadline that actually does something

A plan has expiration_days, but that's when a buyer's membership expires after purchase — not when sales stop. There is no "entries close at" concept anywhere in the model, and nothing that automatically halts sales or kicks off a draw at a point in time. You can stash a date in metadata, but Whop will never read it.

2. Tickets as entries, not as line items

A plan models one fixed-price entry. Real raffles let someone buy ten tickets in one go, cap how many any single person can hold, and show live odds. Plans have an allow_multiple_quantity flag, but no per-user cap and no notion of odds. "10 out of 1,000" isn't a quantity multiplier — it's a different object.

3. A draw people can trust

This is the big one. There is no winner-selection service in the codebase — no RNG, no drawing, nothing. And for a raffle, "pick a random row" isn't good enough. Buyers handing over money for odds need verifiable fairness: a seed from a signed external beacon, a published hash, an immutable audit record anyone can check. Without it, it's indistinguishable from a scam.

The shape of the fix: make raffle a first-class object. A POST /raffles primitive owns the price, the supply, the per-user cap, the ends_at, and the draw mode. A close worker fires at the deadline. POST /raffles/{id}/tickets handles multi-entry buys with odds, POST /raffles/{id}/draw does the provably-fair selection, and GET /raffles/{id}/entries makes the odds public. 
// the primitive that gives the deadline and the draw a home
POST /raffles
{
  "company_id": "biz_…", "image_id": "file_…",
  "ticket_price": "5", "total_tickets": 1000,
  "max_tickets_per_user": 50,
  "ends_at": "2026-07-01T00:00:00Z",
  "winner_count": 1, "draw_mode": "auto_on_close"
}
→ { "id": "raf_…", "checkout_url": "…", "status": "open" }

Three-quarters of a raffle is commerce we already ship. The last quarter — deadline, odds, a fair draw — is the whole reason anyone calls it a raffle.

The two genuinely new dependencies

Most of what a raffle needs externally, we already pay for: PSPs for payments, Sumsub for KYC, EasyPost for shipping, the ads image-and-copy AI pipeline for the prize page. The draw is the exception. Verifiable randomness is a real integration decision — a signed random.org response or a drand beacon is cheap and off-chain; an on-chain VRF is maximally trustworthy but heavier to operate. The second new dependency is conditional: jurisdiction-gated age/eligibility verification, only if compliance requires it per region.

The thing that isn't an endpoint

Raffles are legally regulated, and the platform already knows it. The matching classification, sweepstakes_raffles, is flagged FINANCING_RESTRICTED and NON_DISCOVERABLE in the backend — hidden from Discover, no buy-now-pay-later. There's no clean public field to even set that classification today. Before a single line of /raffles ships, legal owns the question of which jurisdictions we launch in and what eligibility rules apply.

That's the real lesson of the exercise. The engineering is tractable — one primitive, four actions, one new integration. The gating risk on a new business type like this isn't the API surface. It's everything around it: regulation, fairness people can verify, and trust we have to earn before we ask anyone to buy a ticket.

Recommendation

Build the raffle as a first-class resource rather than bolting metadata onto plans. The deadline and the draw have nowhere else to live, and faking them in metadata pushes core behavior off-platform where we can't guarantee fairness or timing. Start whop.com-only behind the compliance review; expose /raffles to third-party developers once the legal and randomness stories are settled. Reuse everything else exactly as-is.
